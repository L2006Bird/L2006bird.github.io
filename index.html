Tom Thomson, J. E. H. MacDonald, Arthur Lismer, Frederick Varley, Frank Johnston and Franklin Carmichael met as employees of the design firm Grip Ltd. in Toronto. In 1913, they were joined by A. Y. Jackson and Lawren Harris. They often met at the Arts and Letters Club of Toronto to discuss their opinions and share their art. This group received monetary support from Harris (heir to the Massey-Harris farm machinery fortune) (1) and Dr. James MacCallum. Harris and MacCallum jointly built the Studio Building in 1914 in the Rosedale ravine to serve as a meeting and working place for the new Canadian art movement. Jackson enlisted in June 1915 and served in France from November 1915 to 1917. Carmichael, MacDonald, Thomson, Varley and Johnston remained in Toronto and struggled in the depressed wartime economy. A further blow to the group came in 1917 when Thomson died mysteriously while canoeing in Algonquin Park. The circumstances of his death remain unclear. As early as 1919, the Group of Seven decided to become a group dedicated to the development of a distinct Canadian art form, since there was none at the time. Although Harris' exactly why these seven men were chosen is unknown, it is believed to have been him. The National Gallery's director at that time, Eric Brown, consistently supported and encouraged them as they prepared for their first exhibition in 1920. A. J. Casson joined the organization in 1926 some years after Frank Johnston moved to Winnipeg in the fall of 1921. Many years earlier, Franklin Carmichael had taken a liking to Casson's sketches and urged him to pursue his artistic passion. (2) The members of the Group began to travel elsewhere in Canada for inspiration, including British Columbia, Quebec, Nova Scotia, and the Arctic. Within a short period of time, the Group decided that some members needed to be outside of Toronto in order to qualify as a "national school of painters."

The Canadian artists developed an approach that was fundamentally different from any ever seen before - an idealistic representation of Canada. As part of their work, they have a strong nationalist aesthetic, and the Group came to public attention around the time of Canada's post-First World War surge of national sentiment.(4) The viewpoint of the conservative establishment was not representative of the overall opinion of the population. Additionally, the controversy surrounding the Group contributed to their reputation as it created a public discourse about Canadian art that had not existed before. In addition to bringing new ideas about artistic value and what it meant to be Canadian, there was great excitement and positive and negative criticism that were encouraged. The Group was convinced that indifference would hamper the emergence of the Canadian art scene. A national Canadian artistic identity can be traced back to the Group of Seven. It is often regarded as an integral part of the new nationality being developed in Canada during the twentieth century because of the attention paid to the Canadian landscape and paintings.

Members of the Group of Seven numbered at least ten over the course of their lifetimes. Tom Thomson, for example, whose death in Algonquin Park in 1917 accounts for some of the Group's controversy, could not be considered an official member. In spite of their eponymous name, all the Group of Seven members were male. Robert Borden, the Prime Minister of Canada, was among the thousands of people who attended the exhibit on the first day alone. The British Empire Exhibition was the biggest and most popular exhibition ever organized by the English government. It showcased industry, engineering, and art from all over the British Empire. As far as Canada was concerned, the job of picking Canadian art submissions sparked a major battle between the established, successful artists and the group of seven who were young, modernist artists. The museums and government bodies they created paved the way for artists to follow. The Group of Seven dominated Canadian art and was recognized internationally. Members were united in their belief that a distinct Canadian art could develop through direct contact with the country's vast and unique landscape. They showed influences of Impressionism, Naturalism, and Art Nouveau in their paintings, but stylistically the Group was a loose-knit group linked rather by their desire to examine and establish permanent settlements in the wilderness of Canada. In contrast, the Group of Seven aimed to protect (through their art) the untouched landscape of their country. Canadians were able to harness a new patriotic spirit as a coping mechanism to quash the cultural superiority of European culture over North American imitations. Their works are characterized by vivid colors, tactile paint handling, and powerful, delicate forms that mark their work as a group of modern artists. The Group of Seven formed in 1920, and they disbanded in 1933. Their paintings mainly depict dense forest panoramas. Almost all of the earlier 20th century's great painters had been influenced by them, including Jack Bush, the Painters Eleven, and Scottish painter Peter Doig, in addition to Tom Thomson, David Milne, and Emily Carr. At the design firm Grip Ltd. in Toronto, Tom Thomson, AY Jackson, E.H. MacDonald, Frank Johnston, Frederick Varley and Franklin Carmichael met for their first time. They frequently gathered at the Arts and Letters Club of Toronto artwork was often shared and ideas were discussed. Dr. McCullum and Harris provided them with financial assistance. During WW1, Jackson and Varley became official war artists. The result of Thomson's mysterious death while in Algonquin Park caused the group to suffer a major setback in 1917. In addition to traveling throughout Ontario, the seven members of the group also sketched the landscapes and developed methods to depict them. 1919, the group decided to form a group devoted to a distinctive Canadian style of art that had not yet arisen, and started calling themselves the Group of Seven. Through Eric Brown, the director of the National Gallery, they were prepared for their first exhibition by 1920 A. J. Casson was invited to join Frank Johnston in Winnipeg in 1926 when he moved there in the fall of 1921. After the 1920 exhibition, the Group was acclaimed as the pioneers of a new Canadian school of art, but reviews of the show initially were mixed. The members of the Group begun to travel throughout Canada and sought inspiration. The group soon dubbed itself a "national school of painters" after being inspired by Samuel Gurney Cresswell and other painters on Royal Navy expeditions. They encouraged and invited painters from outside Toronto, eventually leading to Edwin Holgate. In 1930, Louise FitzGerald from Montreal, Quebec, and LeMoine FitzGerald from Winnipeg, Manitoba, joined. The Canadian Group of Painters was formed as a result of the group's widesoread influence in 1931 and following MacDonald's passing in 1932. This association would become the first fully national association of painters formed since 1878 with painters meeting in British Columbia.
